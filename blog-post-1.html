The Ancient History of Poke in Hawaii

Home › Blog
By Kai NakamuraJune 15, 20256 min read

Long before poke became a global food trend, it was a humble staple of Hawaiian fishermen. The word "poke" (pronounced poh-keh) means "to slice or cut crosswise" in Hawaiian — a description of how raw fish was prepared for immediate consumption at sea.

Ancient Origins

Archaeological evidence and oral histories suggest that ancient Hawaiians were eating raw fish preparations for over a thousand years before Western contact. Ancient poke — called lawalu in some traditions — was typically seasoned with sea salt (pa'akai), crushed kukui nuts (inamona), and limu (seaweed), all harvested directly from the Hawaiian reef ecosystem.

Hawaiian fishermen would prepare poke directly on their outrigger canoes, eating it as a high-protein, energizing meal while fishing. There was no soy sauce, no sesame oil, no avocado — those are all 20th-century additions brought by immigrant communities.

The Japanese Influence

Hawaii's plantation era brought tens of thousands of Japanese immigrants to the islands from the 1880s onward. With them came a deep culinary tradition of sashimi, shoyu (soy sauce), sesame, and pickled ginger. These ingredients were gradually incorporated into poke, creating the shoyu-marinated ahi poke that most people recognize today.

By the mid-20th century, poke had become an essential fixture at fish markets, party platters, and Hawaiian family gatherings. Every supermarket fish counter in Hawaii carried several varieties, sold by the pound.

Poke Goes Mainland

The poke explosion on the US mainland happened rapidly from about 2012 onward, driven by social media imagery of colorful, photogenic bowls. What had been a modest Hawaiian fish market staple suddenly became a $3 billion industry with thousands of "poke bowl" restaurants opening across the country.

Opinions vary among Hawaii locals about this export. Some are proud that their food tradition is appreciated globally. Others feel the mainland versions — with artificial ingredients, substandard fish, and inflated prices — bear little resemblance to the real thing.

What Authentic Poke Looks Like

At Poke Wave, we return to fundamentals: fresh fish sourced that morning, minimal seasoning that enhances rather than overpowers, and a respect for the Hawaiian origins of the dish. Our Hawaiian-style ahi bowl — with inamona, limu, sea salt, and green onion — is the closest we can get to what ancient Hawaiian fishermen ate on their canoes.

Next time you pick up your bowl, take a moment to appreciate the thousand-year history in your hands.
